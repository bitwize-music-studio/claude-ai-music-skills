# Classical

## Genre Overview

Classical music encompasses centuries of instrumental and orchestral traditions, characterized by complex harmonic structures, sophisticated compositional techniques, and emotional depth. From the mathematical precision of Baroque counterpoint to the experimental soundscapes of modern classical, this genre emphasizes technical mastery, instrumental virtuosity, and timeless musical innovation.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Orchestral (strings, woodwinds, brass, percussion), piano, chamber ensembles, solo instruments
- **Vocals**: Typically instrumental, though vocal elements appear in opera and choral works
- **Production**: Acoustic recording, dynamic range, emphasis on natural instrument tones
- **Energy**: Varies from delicate and intimate to grand and dramatic
- **Structure**: Sonata form, theme and variations, fugue, concerto movements, symphonic development

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Baroque | Ornate, contrapuntal, harpsichord-driven (1600-1750) | Bach, Vivaldi, Handel |
| Classical Period | Balanced, structured, symphony-focused (1750-1820) | Mozart, Beethoven, Haydn |
| Romantic | Expressive, emotional, virtuosic (1820-1900) | Chopin, Brahms, Wagner |
| Modern/Contemporary | Experimental, atonal, minimalist (1900-present) | John Williams, Hans Zimmer, Karlheinz Stockhausen |
| Orchestral | Large ensemble, symphonic, cinematic | Tchaikovsky, Debussy, John Williams |
| Chamber Music | Small ensemble (2-10 musicians), intimate | Mozart quartets, Brahms chamber works |
| Piano Solo | Solo keyboard, virtuosic or intimate | Chopin, Liszt, Rachmaninoff |
