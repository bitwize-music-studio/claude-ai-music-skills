# CHILLWAVE

## Genre Overview

Chillwave emerged in 2009 as a distinctly internet-native genre, crystallizing on music blogs like Gorilla vs. Bear, Pitchfork, and Altered Zones. The term itself was initially satirical - coined by Carles of the blog Hipster Runoff to mock the proliferation of micro-genres - but quickly became the accepted label for a wave of bedroom producers creating hazy, nostalgic electronic pop. The holy trinity of early chillwave - Washed Out (Ernest Greene), Toro y Moi (Chazwick Bundick), and Neon Indian (Alan Palomo) - all released breakthrough material within months of each other in 2009-2010, establishing the genre's sonic vocabulary: washed-out synths, heavily reverbed vocals, lo-fi production, and an unmistakable yearning for a half-remembered past.

The genre's lo-fi bedroom aesthetic was both philosophical choice and practical necessity. These artists recorded on modest equipment - often just a laptop, a cheap synthesizer, and free software - deliberately embracing tape hiss, compression artifacts, and imperfect sound quality as textural elements rather than flaws. The production style evoked degraded VHS tapes, faded Polaroids, and childhood memories viewed through a gauzy filter. This wasn't retro in the way synthwave would later be retro (faithfully recreating 80s sounds); instead, chillwave filtered 80s and early 90s pop through layers of fog, creating something that felt like nostalgia itself rather than a specific past era.

By 2012, critics declared chillwave "dead," but its influence proved enduring. The genre's DNA spread into bedroom pop, vaporwave, lo-fi hip-hop beats, and indie electronic music broadly. Artists like Washed Out and Toro y Moi evolved beyond the label's constraints while maintaining its emotional core, proving that chillwave's introspective approach to electronic music had lasting resonance. The genre represents a pivotal moment when internet culture, DIY production, and millennial nostalgia converged to create something genuinely new - music that sounds like scrolling through Tumblr at 2 AM, feeling simultaneously connected and isolated.

## Characteristics

- **Instrumentation**: Analog synths (Juno-60, Korg Polysix), vintage drum machines, lo-fi percussion, occasionally sparse guitar or bass, minimal live instrumentation, extensive use of samples from 80s-90s pop
- **Vocals**: Treated heavily with reverb/echo, washed-out or ethereal delivery, often buried in the mix as another textural element rather than focal point, dreamy and detached emotional register
- **Production**: Deliberately lo-fi or degraded sound quality, warm tape saturation, compressed dynamics, heavy use of effects processing, VHS-quality audio artifacts, gated reverb on drums, sidechain compression for breathing effect
- **Energy/Mood**: Relaxed, introspective, melancholic, nostalgic, dreamy, peaceful yet subtly emotional, bittersweet, hazy, sun-drenched yet melancholy
- **Structure**: Minimalist song structures, long instrumental intros/outros, looping rhythmic elements, gradual builds rather than dramatic shifts, hypnotic repetition, fade-out endings

## Lyric Conventions

- **Default rhyme scheme**: Loose or none. Dreamy, nostalgia-soaked.
- **Rhyme quality**: Incidental. Hazy vocals with lo-fi processing blur individual words.
- **Verse structure**: Sparse â€” short phrases with reverb and delay. Retro synths dominate.
- **Key rule**: Nostalgia and haze. Dreamy, blissed-out. Vocals are part of the texture.
- **Avoid**: Clear vocal delivery. Dense wordplay. Narratives that fight the dreamy wash.
- **Density/pacing (Suno)**: Default **2-4 lines/verse** at 80-110 BPM. Hazy, nostalgic. Heavily processed vocals. Simple phrases, dreamy imagery. Topics: 1/verse.

## Subgenres & Styles

| Style | Description | Reference Artists |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Glo-Fi** | Glowing, uplifting variant with more bass and rhythmic drive; "glowing" synths with wider hooks; brighter and more optimistic than standard chillwave | Neon Indian, Memory Cassette, Small Black |
| **Hypnagogic Pop** | Trippy, oneiric take on pop songwriting; lyrics drift in and out of focus; more experimental structure; named after the hypnagogic state between waking and sleep | Ariel Pink, Oneohtrix Point Never, James Ferraro |
| **Dream Pop Electronic** | Electronic interpretation of dream pop; ethereal vocals layered heavily in reverb; lush synth arrangements that blur the line between shoegaze and electronic | Purity Ring, Beach House (electronic moments), Wild Nothing |
| **Synthwave Chill** | Retro 80s synth vibes with more uptempo energy; neon-noir aesthetics with chilled delivery; more rhythmically driven than pure chillwave | Com Truise, The Midnight, FM-84 |
| **Chillgaze** | Blend of chillwave and shoegaze; distorted guitars layered under hazy synths; ethereal vocals submerged in walls of sound; more guitar-forward | Tycho, Tame Impala (early Innerspeaker era), DIIV |
| **Vapor Chill** | Ambient, drift-focused variant; extremely minimalist; sample-based; influenced by early vaporwave aesthetics; more conceptual and art-focused | Blank Banshee, James Ferraro, early Macintosh Plus |
| **Lo-Fi Bedroom Pop** | Chillwave's DIY ethos applied to more traditional pop songwriting; intimate, confessional lyrics over warm, imperfect production | Mac DeMarco (early), Homeshake, Men I Trust |
| **Tropical Chillwave** | Incorporates tropical and balearic elements; steel drums, marimbas, and island rhythms filtered through chillwave's hazy production style | Washed Out (Paracosm era), Poolside, Kraak & Smaak |

## Reference Tracks

- **Washed Out - "Feel It All Around"** - The chillwave anthem that launched a thousand bedroom producers. Ernest Greene's breakthrough track opens with a sample from Gary Low's "I Want You" filtered into oblivion, layering hazy synths over a hypnotic beat. The Portlandia theme song placement cemented its cultural ubiquity. Note how the vocals are nearly unintelligible, functioning as texture rather than lyrical content.

- **Toro y Moi - "Blessa"** - Lo-fi funk-influenced chillwave with layered synths and introspective delivery. Chazwick Bundick's debut single demonstrates chillwave's debt to R&B and disco, with chopped vocal samples and a bassline that actually grooves. More rhythmically sophisticated than much of the genre.

- **Neon Indian - "Deadbeat Summer"** - Alan Palomo's breakout track encapsulates the genre's bittersweet nostalgia. The arpeggiated synths and distorted vocals evoke endless summer afternoons that never quite existed. Brighter and more upbeat than typical chillwave while maintaining the hazy production aesthetic.

- **Neon Indian - "Polish Girl"** - From Era Extrana, showing Neon Indian's evolution toward cleaner, more synth-pop-oriented production. The vocoder-treated chorus and driving rhythm point toward where chillwave alumni would head post-genre.

- **Com Truise - "Fluxland"** - Instrumental chillwave with cinematic 80s synth arrangements and moody atmosphere. Seth Haley's production demonstrates how the genre could work without vocals, creating narratives through texture and melody alone. The side-chained bass and gated snares evoke John Carpenter soundtracks.

- **Tycho - "A Walk"** - Sophisticated chillwave blend with jazz-influenced harmony and lush ambient textures. Scott Hansen's instrumental approach elevates the genre toward post-rock territory, with live guitar loops layered over electronic production. Represents chillwave's more mature, album-oriented direction.

- **Memory Cassette - "Asleep at a Party"** - Deeply nostalgic lo-fi chillwave with organic synth textures and subtle beats. The track captures the genre's emotional essence: the feeling of being present but dissociated, surrounded by people but alone with your thoughts.

- **Ariel Pink - "Round and Round"** - From Before Today, this hypnagogic pop gem predates and influences chillwave proper. Ariel Pink's maximalist lo-fi production and twisted 80s pop sensibility created a template others would simplify. More structured than typical chillwave but sharing its nostalgic DNA.

- **Small Black - "Despicable Dogs"** - One of chillwave's most dance-floor-friendly moments. The Brooklyn quartet brought more traditional song structure and cleaner production to the genre, pointing toward synth-pop crossover potential.

- **Wild Nothing - "Chinatown"** - Dream pop meets chillwave in Jack Tatum's jangly, reverb-soaked indie. More guitar-forward than synth-centric peers, demonstrating how chillwave's aesthetic could translate to rock instrumentation.

- **Blackbird Blackbird - "Pure"** - San Francisco producer Mikey Maramag's bright, tropical-tinged chillwave. More optimistic than the melancholic norm, with steel drum samples and island rhythms filtered through the genre's characteristic haze.

- **Teen Daze - "Let's Fall Asleep Together"** - Ambient-leaning chillwave that verges on beatless soundscape. Jamison Isaak's intimate production creates music for headphones in dark rooms, pushing the genre toward its dreamiest extreme.

- **Craft Spells - "After the Moment"** - Justin Vallesteros channels 80s new wave through chillwave production. The result splits the difference between Depeche Mode and Washed Out, with actual hooks beneath the reverb wash.

- **Memoryhouse - "Sleep Patterns"** - Cinematic chillwave with orchestral sweep. The duo's approach brought more emotional grandeur to the bedroom aesthetic, suggesting chillwave could scale up without losing its intimate core.
